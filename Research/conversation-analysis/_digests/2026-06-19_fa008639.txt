## What's actually breaking

DWService isn't your problem — it's a cloud relay that tunnels out, so it stays up. What fails "randomly" is **ROS 2 discovery**, and on a TurtleBot 4 over enterprise WiFi (your NUwave setup) there are two classic causes:

1. **DDS multicast discovery over managed WiFi.** Default ROS 2 (Fast DDS "simple discovery") relies on multicast to find peers. Enterprise/eduroam-style networks like NUwave routinely **block multicast and enable client isolation**, and re-associations/roaming drop discovery packets. Result: `ros2 topic list` sometimes works, sometimes returns nothing or hangs — exactly the "random" behavior you describe.

2. **Changing DHCP IP + un-sourced shells.** Each reconnect the robot can get a new IP, and a fresh DWService terminal often isn't a login shell, so `/opt/ros/.../setup.bash` and your `ROS_DOMAIN_ID`/`RMW` env aren't loaded → ROS looks "gone."

## Why it happens

DWService's agent is installed as a **system service that runs as root**. So when you open its *Shell* (or a desktop session that isn't logged into your account), you land as **root** — or at least not in your normal user's login environment. Your SSH session, by contrast, logs you in as your normal user with a **login shell** that sources that user's `.profile`/`.bashrc`.

Everything that makes ROS "work" lives in your normal user, not root:
- `source ~/ros2_ws/install/setup.bash`, `ROS_DOMAIN_ID`, `RMW_IMPLEMENTATION` etc. that are set in **your user's** `.bashrc`
- the **workspace files** under your home dir
- **group memberships** (`dialout`, `video`, `tty`) for serial/USB access
- ROS daemon / DDS shared-memory segments are **per-user**, so a root shell can't even see the nodes your user's daemon started

That's the "random" part: when you happen to be in your user's context it works; when DWService drops you as root, ROS looks gone.

## The reliable fix

**Always switch into your normal user with a *login* shell** as the first thing in any DWService shell:

```bash
sudo -iu <youruser>      # -i = login shell, full env, correct groups
# or:  su - <youruser>
```

The `-i` / `-` is the key — it reproduces exactly what SSH does (sources profile, sets env, applies group membership). Plain `su <user>` or `sudo -u <user>` will **not** fix it because they keep the non-login environment.
